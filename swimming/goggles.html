Goggles are the most personal piece of swimming equipment you'll own. The right pair creates a watertight seal against your specific face shape, provides the UV or anti-glare protection you need for your environment, and gives you the field of vision to swim efficiently. Three factors determine fit: gasket softness (harder = more durable, softer = more comfortable), lens size (larger = wider view and easier to adjust), and nose bridge (adjustable vs fixed). Never skip a proper suction test before buying.

How to Choose Swimming Goggles

Face Shape & Seal

The most important factor is whether the goggle seals against your face without relying entirely on strap tension. Press the lens gently to your eye socket — a good goggle holds briefly from suction alone. If it doesn't seal without a strap, it'll leak in the pool regardless of how tight you set the band.

Lens Type

Clear lenses work best indoors under artificial lighting. Smoke or tinted lenses reduce glare in brighter pools. Mirrored lenses are best for outdoor swimming or bright indoor facilities. For open-water racing or triathlons, always choose mirrored or heavily tinted.

Swedish vs Standard

Swedish goggles (hard frame, no gasket) have lower drag and a more stable fit once adjusted — ideal for competitive swimmers. Standard goggles with soft silicone gaskets are far more comfortable for long training sessions and casual swimmers.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I stop my goggles from fogging up?

New goggles come with a factory anti-fog coating — never rub the inside of the lenses or you'll remove it. Rinse with cold water after each swim and let them air dry. Once the coating wears off (usually after several months), use anti-fog spray or drops before each session.

How tight should swimming goggles be?

The suction should hold the goggles to your face for a second or two without the strap. If they leave deep red marks that hurt, they're too tight. If they leak immediately, they're too loose or the gasket shape doesn't match your face.

What's the difference between Swedish and standard goggles?

Swedish goggles (like TYR Socket Rockets) are minimal — small hard plastic lenses with no gasket. They give a very low profile and wide field of view but can be uncomfortable for beginners. Standard goggles have soft silicone gaskets that seal around the eye socket for more comfort.

Do I need mirrored or tinted lenses?

Mirrored lenses are ideal for outdoor swimming in sunlight — they reduce glare significantly. Clear lenses are best for indoor pools with artificial lighting. Tinted lenses split the difference. For open water, mirrored or smoke-tinted is the right call.
