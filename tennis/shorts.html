Tennis Shorts Buying Guide

Pockets are non-negotiable

Tennis shorts live and die by their pockets — you need deep, reinforced pockets that hold a second ball securely while you serve and don't spill it when you sprint. This is the single biggest difference between tennis shorts and generic gym shorts.

Length and freedom of movement

The modern standard is a 7-inch inseam — above the knee, full range of motion. Taller players sometimes prefer 9-inch. What matters is zero restriction on lunges and serves: look for stretch wovens or side splits.

Fabric and the waistband

Lightweight stretch polyester dries fast and moves with you. A flat elastic waistband with internal drawcord stays put through long matches — bulky waistbands and zips chafe when you're soaked in the third set.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I play tennis in gym or basketball shorts?

You can, but ball pockets are the problem — gym shorts have shallow or zipped pockets that won't hold a second ball during serves. Dedicated tennis shorts fix this for the same price.

What inseam length is best for tennis shorts?

7 inches is the modern standard — above the knee with full freedom of movement. Go 9 inches if you're tall or prefer more coverage; avoid anything past the knee, which restricts lunges.

How many balls should fit in a tennis short pocket?

At least one, comfortably and securely, while you move. Good tennis shorts hold a ball through sprints without bouncing it out — test this before committing to a brand.

Why do some tennis shorts have compression liners?

Built-in liners add support and chafe protection and replace separate underwear. Some players love them, others find them restrictive — they're a preference, not a requirement.
